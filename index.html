How we calculate tuition

Tuition figures are sourced from official 2025-26 university pages. Booth MBA/MPCS charges a higher per-quarter rate than the standard MBA ($32,028 vs $29,118) to cover up to 2,400 coursework units. Kellogg MMM has the same per-quarter rate as the 2Y MBA ($28,790) but includes an extra summer quarter in Year 1.

  • Booth Y1: $96,084 tuition + $4,690 fees (services $1,509 + admin $3,100 + transcript $81)
  • Booth Y2: $92,619 tuition + $1,509 fees (services only, continuing rate)
  • Kellogg Y1: $115,160 tuition (4 qtrs incl. summer) + $5,091 fees
  • Kellogg Y2: $86,370 tuition (3 qtrs, same as standard 2Y) + $2,512 fees

Scholarships are split proportionally by quarters per year. Booth: 3/6 per year. Kellogg: 4/7 in Y1 (summer quarter), 3/7 in Y2.

How we calculate loan costs

US Loan (Earnest): Standard amortization. Monthly payment M = P × r(1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n - 1) where P = principal, r = monthly rate, n = total payments. Earnest charges zero origination fees, late fees, or prepayment penalties.

Indian Bank Loan: More complex because of currency conversion costs and FX depreciation.

  • Principal: Converted to INR at the current spot rate
  • Processing fee: % of INR principal + 18% GST on the fee
  • TCS: 0.5% tax collected at source on amounts exceeding ₹7,00,000 (refundable in tax filing)
  • Disbursement costs: Each quarterly tranche incurs forex markup (% of tranche) + SWIFT fee ($20) + correspondent bank fee ($25)
  • EMI: Computed in INR on (principal + processing fee) using standard amortization
  • USD-equivalent cost: Each month's INR EMI is divided by the projected FX rate: EMI₹ / (spot × (1 + depreciation)^(month/12)). As INR weakens, each EMI costs fewer USD — this is the FX advantage for USD earners
  • Effective total: Sum of all USD-equivalent EMIs + disbursement costs + TCS

Year-over-year view: When "Year 1" or "Year 2" is selected, the loan principal = that year's tuition + fees - proportional scholarship + living costs for that year's duration. Kellogg Y1 is notably higher due to the summer quarter.

How we calculate the breakeven

The chart sweeps the Indian loan interest rate from 4% to 15% in 0.25% steps. At each rate, it computes the total USD-equivalent cost of the Indian loan (including all fees, FX conversion, and depreciation effects) and plots it against the fixed US loan total cost.

Three curves are shown for different INR depreciation assumptions (your selected rate ±1.5%) to show how sensitive the breakeven is to FX movement.

Early payoff: When the payoff period is shorter than the loan term, both loans are computed as: regular EMI for the payoff period + lump-sum payment of the remaining balance. This reduces total interest for both loans but affects them differently — the Indian loan's FX advantage shrinks with shorter payoff since there's less time for depreciation to compound.

The exact breakeven rate is found using bisection (binary search) to 0.01% precision.

How we calculate post-tax income

Take-home pay is computed by deducting three layers of tax from gross salary:

  • Federal income tax: 2025 brackets with standard deduction ($14,600 single). Progressive rates from 10% to 37%
  • FICA: Social Security 6.2% (capped at $168,600) + Medicare 1.45% (no cap) + Additional Medicare 0.9% above $200k
  • Illinois state tax: Flat 4.95% on taxable income

Disposable income = post-tax monthly income - monthly living expenses. This is what goes toward loan repayment (plus any extra payment you set).

Loan Payoff Trajectory
How we simulate the payoff

Month-by-month simulation starting from graduation. Each month:

  • Salary grows annually at the growth rate you set
  • Post-tax income is computed, then living expenses are subtracted
  • All remaining disposable income (+ any extra payment) goes toward the loan
  • Interest accrues on the remaining balance at the loan rate
  • For Indian loans: interest accrues in INR but your payments are in USD, so each month's cost is adjusted for depreciation

If your disposable income can't cover the monthly interest, the balance grows and a warning is shown. The simulation caps at 30 years.

The red area shows the declining loan balance (left axis). The green line shows cumulative amount paid (right axis).

Net Worth Trajectory
How we calculate net worth

Net worth = negative loan balance while repaying, then accumulated savings after payoff. The "Break Even" line at $0 marks when you've fully paid off the loan. After that, your disposable income accumulates as savings (simplified — no investment returns modeled).

The simulation runs 24 months past loan payoff to show the trajectory of savings accumulation. The green dot marks the exact break-even month.

Salary Sensitivity Analysis
How we run the sensitivity analysis

Runs the same payoff simulation at five salary levels: your selected salary ±$20k and ±$40k. All other parameters (growth rate, living expenses, loan terms) stay the same.

The solid line is your current salary selection. Dashed lines show the alternatives. This helps you see how much salary matters — often a $20k salary difference changes payoff time by 1-2 years.

How we project cash flow

Each row aggregates the month-by-month simulation into annual totals. Gross income grows at your set rate. Total tax = federal + FICA + Illinois state. Living expenses = monthly amount × 12. Loan payment = sum of all monthly payments that year. Balance = remaining loan at year-end.

Note: this assumes you start working immediately after graduation and allocate all disposable income to loan repayment. In practice, you may have a signing bonus, relocation costs, or other one-time events not modeled here.
